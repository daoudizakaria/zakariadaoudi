    Introduction

    The objective of this simulation tool is to build the mathematics required to model a pipeline with and without cathodic protection. Cathodic protection is a proven method for preventing corrosion on metal structures and is widely used to protect pipelines, ships, offshore installations, and more.

    In this tool, we focus on the Impressed Current Cathodic Protection (ICCP) system. The soil is discretized into a lattice of N×M cells (each 10×10 m²), and the pipeline within each cell has a given length L. This approach allows us to simulate current density and the associated electric fields along the pipeline.

    ICCP Mathematics

    The mathematical framework for the ICCP simulation tool comprises several key areas:

    1. Anode Mathematics: Calculation of anode resistance (horizontal and vertical), positioning, current output, and number of anodes required.
    2. Basic Circuit Mathematics: Total circuit resistance, including structure-to-electrolyte and wire resistance.
    3. Rectifier Part: Determination of rectifier voltage and current.
    4. Holiday Mathematics: Estimation of corrosion rate and corrosion current density relative to holiday size.
    5. Pipe-to-Soil Potential: Estimation of the potential difference between the pipeline and the soil.
    6. Coatings: Calculation of coating conductance, attenuation factor, and required protective current.

    Anode Mathematics

    For horizontal anodes, the resistance is calculated as:

    $$R_{h} = 0.00521\,\rho\,L \left[2.303\left(\log_{10}\frac{4L}{D} + \log_{10}\frac{L}{h}\right) + \frac{2h}{L} - 2\right] \quad (1)$$

    Here, L is the anode-pipeline distance, D is the anode diameter, and h is determined by:

    $$h = d_{p} + L\tan\alpha \quad (2)$$

    where \(d_{p}\) is the pipeline depth (assumed 1.5 m) and \(\alpha\) is the angle of inclination.

    Basic Circuit Mathematics

    The total circuit resistance is:

    $$R_{T} = R_{a} + R_{W} + R_{C} \quad (3)$$

    where \(R_{a}\) is the anode resistance, \(R_{W}\) is the wire resistance, and \(R_{C}\) is the structure-to-electrolyte resistance.

    Rectifier Part

    The rectifier voltage is computed as:

    $$V_{rec} = I_{RT} \times 150\% \quad (4)$$

    with \(I_{RT}\) being the total required current.

    Pipe-to-Soil Potential

    The potential difference between the pipe and the soil can be approximated as:

    $$\Delta V = -\frac{Q}{A\epsilon}\left[d-t\left(1-\frac{1}{1+K_e}\right)\right] \quad (5)$$

    where \(Q\) is the total charge, \(A\) is the cross-sectional area, \(\epsilon\) is the permittivity, \(d\) is the distance from the pipe to the measurement point, \(t\) is the pipe diameter, and \(K_e\) is the electric susceptibility.

    Coatings and Protective Current

    The pipe resistance is given by:

    $$R_{p} = \frac{\rho}{\pi \times L \times (OD - WT)} \quad (6)$$

    The coating conductance is:

    $$G = \frac{\pi \times OD}{\omega} \quad (7)$$

    The attenuation constant is:

    $$\alpha = \sqrt{R_{p} \times G} \quad (8)$$

    And the required protective current can be approximated by:

    $$I_{0} \approx \frac{\Delta E}{R_{CR} \tan (\alpha \times L)} \quad (9)$$

    where \(R_{CR} = \sqrt{\frac{R_{p}}{G}}\) is the pipe characteristic resistance.

    ICCP Algorithm

    The following steps outline the algorithm used in the ICCP simulation tool:

    1. Input: Measure the pipe-to-soil potential \(E\) and compute the voltage drop \(\Delta E = -0.85 - E\).
    2. Determine the pipe resistance \(R\) (e.g., \(R = \frac{215.8L}{10^6 W}\), where \(W\) is the pipe weight per meter).
    3. Calculate the current flow using Ohm’s law: \(I = \frac{\Delta E}{R}\).
    4. Determine the pipe surface area \(A_P = \pi d l\) (with \(l\) as the pipe length and \(d\) the outside diameter).
    5. Compute the horizontal anode resistance \(R_h\) using the equation above.
    6. Estimate the anode current output and the number of anodes required.
    7. Calculate the wire resistance \(R_W\) based on the cable’s specified resistance and the wire length.
    8. Determine the pipe-to-soil resistance \(R_{PS} = \frac{\Delta E}{I}\).
    9. Compute the total circuit resistance \(R_T = R_a + R_W + R_{PS}\).
    10. Finally, calculate the required rectifier output \(V_{rec} = I_{RT} \times 150\%\), where \(I_{RT}\) is the total required current.

    Conclusion

    Cathodic protection—especially via the Impressed Current method—is essential for preventing corrosion on pipelines and other metal structures. By applying detailed mathematical models and algorithms, engineers can design effective protection systems. This simulation tool integrates complex parameters—from anode and circuit resistances to protective current requirements—into an accessible framework that aids in system design and optimization.
